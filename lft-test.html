Clinical Insights & Liver Health Management

Understand how each liver marker acts on your body and how it can be medically managed:

SGPT / ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) Primary Liver Enzyme
Clinical Impact:

ALT is an enzyme found primarily inside liver cells. When the liver is damaged or inflamed (due to fatty liver, toxins, or viral hepatitis), these cells rupture, causing ALT to leak into the bloodstream. Spikes in ALT are a direct signal of active liver injury.

How it is Controlled:

Managed by eliminating alcohol consumption, reducing body weight, and minimizing the use of unnecessary over-the-counter medications (like frequent painkillers) that strain the liver. A diet rich in antioxidants helps reduce liver inflammation.

Bilirubin (Total & Fractions) Bile Pigment Tracking
Clinical Impact:

Bilirubin is a yellow waste product created when old red blood cells break down. The liver processes it and excretes it via bile. High levels cause Jaundice (yellowing of eyes and skin), indicating either liver blockage, severe cell dysfunction, or excessive blood cell breakdown.

How it is Controlled:

Treatment depends on the root cause. Staying aggressively hydrated helps flush out toxins. If caused by a bile duct obstruction (like gallstones), surgical clearance is needed; if caused by viral hepatitis, specific antiviral care is required.

ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase) Biliary & Bone Marker
Clinical Impact:

ALP is an enzyme heavily concentrated in the bile ducts of the liver and in bones. High ALP levels typically indicate a blockage in the biliary system (bile ducts) or bone disorders. It shows whether bile fluid is flowing normally from the liver to the intestines.

How it is Controlled:

Controlled by addressing underlying gallbladder or bile duct blockages. Medical management includes low-fat diets to reduce gallbladder workload and medications prescribed by a specialist to improve bile flow.

Liver Function Tests (LFT): A Clinical Overview

Updated: July 2026 | Verified by Laboratory Professionals
What is an LFT Panel? Liver Function Tests (LFTs) are a group of blood tests used to screen for, diagnose, and monitor liver disease or damage. This comprehensive panel measures the levels of specific enzymes and proteins in your blood to evaluate how well your liver is synthesizing, metabolizing, and clearing substances.

1. Bilirubin Markers (Biliary Secretion & Clearance)

Bilirubin is a yellowish pigment formed during the normal breakdown of red blood cells. The liver filters it from the blood. High levels cause Jaundice (yellowing of eyes and skin).

  • 1. Total Bilirubin: The combination of both direct and indirect bilirubin in the blood. Elevated levels indicate liver injury, bile duct blockage, or hemolysis.
  • 2. Direct (Conjugated) Bilirubin: Bilirubin that has been processed by the liver and is water-soluble. High levels often point to structural blockages in the bile ducts or gallstones.
  • 3. Indirect (Unconjugated) Bilirubin: Bilirubin traveling through the bloodstream to the liver. High levels usually indicate excess RBC breakdown (Hemolysis) or genetic disorders like Gilbert's Syndrome.

2. Liver Enzymes (Hepatocellular Injury Indicators)

Enzymes are proteins that help accelerate chemical reactions in the body. When liver cells are stressed or injured, these enzymes spill into the bloodstream.

  • 4. SGPT / ALT (Serum Glutamic Pyruvic Transaminase): Found predominantly in liver cells. This is the most specific marker for liver injury. Marked elevations indicate acute hepatitis, fatty liver, or toxin-induced damage.
  • 5. SGOT / AST (Serum Glutamic Oxaloacetic Transaminase): Found in the liver, but also in heart and skeletal muscles. Elevated levels along with normal SGPT might suggest muscle damage rather than liver issues.
  • 6. ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase): An enzyme related to the bile ducts and bones. Significantly high levels are a classic sign of biliary tract obstruction, cholestasis, or bone-related disorders.
  • 7. GGT (Gamma-Glutamyl Transferase): A highly sensitive enzyme marker for bile duct injury. It is frequently elevated in chronic alcohol consumption, prescription medication overuse, or early biliary disease.

3. Synthetic Function Markers (Proteins)

The liver is the body's primary manufacturing hub for vital blood proteins. Low protein levels indicate chronic liver damage where the liver loses its synthetic capacity.

  • 8. Total Protein: Measures the cumulative amount of two major types of protein in your blood: albumin and globulin. It helps assess nutritional status and systemic diseases.
  • 9. Albumin: The main protein manufactured exclusively by the liver. It keeps fluid from leaking out of blood vessels (preventing Edema/Ascites). Low levels signal chronic liver conditions like Cirrhosis or malnutrition.
  • 10. Globulin: Produced partly by the liver and partly by the immune system (lymph nodes). It plays a crucial role in fighting infections. High levels often indicate chronic inflammation or autoimmune liver disease.
  • 11. A/G Ratio (Albumin to Globulin Ratio): A calculated mathematical ratio of albumin divided by globulin. A low ratio indicates either underproduction of albumin (liver disease) or overproduction of globulin (active infection/autoimmune disease).

Standard Clinical Reference Ranges (Adults)

The following table outlines the standard baseline values for the 11 key parameters of a complete Liver Function Test panel.

Parameter Standard Reference Range Primary Clinical Focus
1. Total Bilirubin 0.2 - 1.2 mg/dL Jaundice & Hemolysis assessment
2. Direct Bilirubin 0.0 - 0.3 mg/dL Bile duct blockage evaluation
3. Indirect Bilirubin 0.2 - 0.8 mg/dL RBC destruction monitoring
4. SGPT (ALT) 7 - 56 U/L Specific liver cell damage marker
5. SGOT (AST) 10 - 40 U/L Liver & Muscle cell health
6. ALP 44 - 147 U/L Bile duct blockages & Bone conditions
7. GGT 9 - 48 U/L Alcohol intake & Bile tract toxicity
8. Total Protein 6.0 - 8.3 g/dL Overall nutritional & systemic status
9. Albumin 3.5 - 5.0 g/dL Chronic liver capacity & Fluid retention
10. Globulin 2.0 - 3.5 g/dL Immune and inflammatory status
11. A/G Ratio 1.1 - 2.5 Synthetic balance diagnostic tool
Important Medical Disclaimer: Mildly elevated liver enzymes do not always signify a critical condition. Transient spikes can happen due to temporary illness, intense exercise, or common medications. All laboratory results must be evaluated comprehensively by a certified medical practitioner.

Complete Blood Count (CBC) Test: A Clinical Overview

Updated: July 2026 | Verified by Laboratory Professionals
What is a CBC? A Complete Blood Count (CBC) is a fundamental diagnostic panel that evaluates the three primary cellular components of blood: red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. It serves as a crucial tool for screening, diagnosing, and monitoring various hematological conditions.

1. Red Blood Cell (RBC) Evaluation & Indices

Red blood cells are responsible for transporting oxygen throughout the body. Evaluating RBCs involves analyzing both total count and specific structural indices:

Total RBC Count & Hemoglobin (Hb)

  • Anemia (Low Hb/RBC): Can result from iron deficiency, chronic diseases, or blood loss, leading to fatigue, pallor, and dyspnea.
  • Polycythemia (High Hb/RBC): Often triggered by chronic hypoxia (e.g., smoking, COPD), dehydration, or primary bone marrow disorders.

RBC Indices (The Breakdown)

  • MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume): Measures the average size of red blood cells. Low MCV indicates microcytic cells (commonly Iron Deficiency Anemia or Thalassemia), while high MCV indicates macrocytic cells (Vitamin B12 or Folate deficiency).
  • MCH (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin): Reflects the average amount of hemoglobin per cell. Crucial for determining if anemia is hypochromic.
  • MCHC (Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration): Evaluates the concentration of hemoglobin within a given volume of packed RBCs.
  • RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width): Measures the variation in RBC size (Anisocytosis). A high RDW is an early indicator of nutritional deficiency anemias.

2. Total & Differential White Blood Cell (WBC) Count

White Blood Cells form the core of the body's immune defense mechanism. A differential count breaks down total WBCs into five distinct types, each responding to specific pathological triggers:

  • Neutrophils: The primary responders to bacterial infections. High counts (Neutrophilia) usually point to acute bacterial issues or tissue inflammation.
  • Lymphocytes: Central to viral immunity and antibody production. Elevated levels (Lymphocytosis) are common in viral infections like mononucleosis or chronic lymphocytic leukemia.
  • Eosinophils: Predominantly active during allergic reactions and parasitic infections (e.g., asthma, worm infestations).

3. Platelet (PLT) Count & Indices

Platelets are essential fragments involved in the primary hemostasis and blood clotting cascade.

  • Thrombocytopenia (Low Platelets): Increases the risk of spontaneous bleeding, petechiae, and bruising. Frequently observed in severe viral infections (such as Dengue), idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP), or bone marrow suppression.
  • Thrombocytosis (High Platelets): Can be reactive (due to acute inflammation or iron deficiency) or clonal, elevating the risk of thrombosis.
  • MPV (Mean Platelet Volume): Indicates the uniform size of platelets; larger platelets often signal increased bone marrow production.

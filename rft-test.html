Renal Function Tests (RFT): A Clinical Overview

Updated: July 2026 | Verified by Laboratory Professionals
What is an RFT Panel? Renal Function Tests (RFTs) are a group of vital investigations performed to evaluate how efficiently the kidneys are filtering metabolic waste and functioning overall.

Detailed Clinical Breakdown

The kidneys act as filtration units. Below is the comprehensive medical detail of each required parameter:

1. Serum Creatinine

A continuous waste byproduct generated at a constant rate by normal muscle breakdown. It is completely filtered out of the blood by healthy kidneys, making it the most important daily baseline marker for renal clearance.

  • High Levels: Indicates decreased kidney function, acute kidney injury (AKI), severe chronic kidney disease (CKD), heavy dehydration, or blockages in the urinary tract.
  • Low Levels: Uncommon; usually found in elderly patients due to low muscle mass, severe malnutrition, or during pregnancy.

2. Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

BUN measures the exact amount of nitrogen in your blood that comes from the waste product urea. It is an excellent parameter for finding out the root cause of filtration delays.

  • High Levels: Points toward congestive heart failure, recent heavy dietary protein intake, gastrointestinal bleeding, or kidney damage.
  • BUN-to-Creatinine Ratio: A high ratio often helps professionals confirm if the patient is suffering from basic fluid loss (dehydration) rather than permanent kidney damage.

3. Serum Urea

Urea is a metabolic waste product synthesized primarily in the liver during protein breakdown. It travels through the blood to the kidneys to be excreted safely.

  • High Levels (Uremia): Occurs when kidneys fail to filter out protein waste effectively, leading to toxic build-up. It can cause fatigue, nausea, and general weakness.
  • Low Levels: Can point to advanced liver disease (since the liver makes urea) or severe overhydration.

4. Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR)

The eGFR is not a separate substance, but a mathematical calculation based on the patient's Serum Creatinine, age, and gender. It is universally recognized as the absolute best way to check kidney efficiency percentage.

  • Normal (> 90 mL/min): Indicates excellent filtration.
  • Low (< 60 mL/min): If sustained for over 3 months, it explicitly confirms Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and tells the clinician how advanced the disease is.

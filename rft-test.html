Kidney Function Tests (RFT / KFT): A Clinical Overview

Updated: July 2026 | Verified by Laboratory Professionals
What is an RFT Panel? Kidney Function Tests (also known as Renal Function Tests or Kidney Function Tests) are a cluster of blood and urine evaluations performed to monitor renal health. The kidneys serve as critical filtration units that eliminate waste materials, manage fluid equilibrium, and control essential electrolytes within the bloodstream.

1. Primary Waste Metabolites (Filtration Efficiency)

These biochemical compounds build up in the vascular system when the kidneys experience decreased filtration capabilities.

  • Serum Creatinine: A cellular waste compound generated through regular skeletal muscle breakdown. Since healthy kidneys filter creatinine entirely from circulation, an elevated baseline serves as a primary indicator of reduced renal performance or acute kidney stress.
  • Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) / Blood Urea: A secondary metabolic byproduct created from dietary protein digestion inside the liver. Elevated values often suggest compromised kidney processing, severe physical dehydration, or high-protein dietary intake.
  • Serum Uric Acid: A metabolic product generated from the degradation of purines. Chronically elevated serum levels can lead to crystallization within joints (Gout) or contribute to stone formations inside the renal pelvis.

2. Functional Cleansing & Filtration Capacity

Evaluating total filtration dynamics helps clinicians categorize structural kidney performance levels correctly.

  • eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate): Widely recognized as the mathematical gold standard for tracking total operational kidney performance. Computed using serum creatinine data alongside patient demographic profiles, a falling eGFR value marks distinct progressive phases of Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD).

3. Serum Electrolytes (Fluid & Acid-Base Equilibrium)

The kidneys regulate specific mineral concentrations strictly to support cellular function and preserve cardiovascular stability.

  • Serum Sodium (Na+): Governs systemic osmotic fluid distribution and blood pressure dynamics. Fluctuations usually point toward altered water balance mechanisms.
  • Serum Potassium (K+): Critically vital for cardiovascular electrical conduction and neuromuscular cellular signaling. Even minor elevations (Hyperkalemia) or deficiencies (Hypokalemia) pose immediate cardiac risks.
  • Serum Chloride (Cl-): Interacts continuously with sodium markers to maintain systemic acid-base equilibrium and fluid balance profiles.

Standard Clinical Reference Ranges (Adults)

The following table maps the standard adult baseline metrics required for a standard diagnostic Renal Function evaluation.

Parameter Standard Reference Range Clinical Significance
Serum Creatinine Male: 0.7 - 1.3 mg/dL
Female: 0.6 - 1.1 mg/dL		 Primary kidney filtration marker
Blood Urea 15 - 45 mg/dL Protein metabolism & hydration status
eGFR > 90 mL/min/1.73 m² Global kidney function staging marker
Serum Uric Acid Male: 3.5 - 7.2 mg/dL
Female: 2.6 - 6.0 mg/dL		 Gout evaluation & stone risk index
Serum Sodium (Na+) 135 - 145 mEq/L Fluid status & osmotic balance
Serum Potassium (K+) 3.5 - 5.1 mEq/L Neuromuscular & cardiac rhythm marker
Serum Chloride (Cl-) 98 - 107 mEq/L Acid-base systemic equilibrium
Important Medical Disclaimer: An isolated drop in eGFR or a temporary elevation in blood urea can happen during acute dehydration episodes and doesn't automatically mean long-term kidney failure. Consistent medical tracking and diagnostic urine screens help map complete renal profiles accurately.
